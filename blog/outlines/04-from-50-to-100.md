# From 50 to 100: What Failing Specs Taught Us About Writing Better Ones

**Target Audience:** Product managers, developers, and scrum teams who write user stories or acceptance criteria; anyone frustrated that their AI-generated code doesn't match intent; teams looking to raise their own spec quality without a lengthy process overhaul.

---

## Hook

The scoring isn't the product. The behavior change is. When SL-026 came back with a 50, we could have argued with the scorer. Instead, we rewrote the spec. The second version scored 100. The implementation took half the time. By the end of Sprint 5, we were writing 100s on the first try — not because the scorer got easier, but because we got better.

## Section 3: The Three Patterns That Kill Scores

Across Sprint 5, the same failure modes appeared repeatedly: vague outcomes ("the user can see the data"), missing actor ("it should redirect"), and untestable assertions ("the experience feels smooth"). Each pattern, one fix.

## Section 4: What a 100-Point Spec Actually Looks Like

Not a template — a real Sprint 5 ticket that scored 100 on the first try, with annotations explaining what made each AC testable, specific, and actionable.

## Section 5: Why the Trend Matters More Than the Score

The goal was never to hit 100. The goal was to write specs that produce clean code. By the end of the sprint, we had both — and the correlation between spec score and implementation quality was hard to ignore.
